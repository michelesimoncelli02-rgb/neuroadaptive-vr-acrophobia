On the day preceding the experiment, participants received an information sheet containing a general overview of the study together with several screenshots of the virtual reality environment (VRE).

Two versions of the Information Sheet were prepared: one for the offline stage and one for the online stage. Each participant received only the version corresponding to the stage in which they took part. 

Prior to the beginning of the experimental procedure, participants were first provided with an informed consent form, which they were required to read and sign before taking part in the study. They were then asked to complete the Fear of Heights Questionnaire (FoHQ). 
Following the completion of the experimental session, participants filled in the Igroup Presence Questionnaire (IPQ) and the Virtual Reality Sickness Questionnaire (VRSQ). 

The information sheets as well as the questionnaires are reported in the Appendix (Section A-F) and they present a brief and easily understandable overview of the experimental procedure, so that also people not used to this type of instrumentations and applications could get the hang of what they were going to undergo. The written consent is based upon these information sheets. 

The Fear of Heights Questionnaire (FoHQ) was administered prior to the experiment to assess the participants' self-reported level of acrophobia. The questionnaire consisted of 12 items evaluating emotional, behavioural, and physiological responses to height-related situations. Participants rated each statement on a five-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (“Strongly Disagree”) to 5 (“Strongly Agree”). A  mean FoHQ score was calculated by averaging the responses across all items, yielding a value between 1 and 5 that facilitates interpretation and comparison with other questionnaire measures.

The Igroup Presence Questionnaire (IPQ) was administered after the virtual reality exposure to assess the participants’ sense of presence within the virtual environment. The questionnaire evaluates several dimensions of presence, including General Presence, Spatial Presence, Involvement, and Experienced Realism. Responses were provided on a seven-point rating scale, and negatively phrased items were reverse-scored prior to analysis. For each subscale, a score was calculated as the mean of the corresponding items. In addition, a global presence score was obtained by averaging all questionnaire items after reverse scoring. Higher scores indicate a stronger sense of presence and immersion in the virtual environment.

The Virtual Reality Sickness Questionnaire (VRSQ) was administered following the VR exposure to evaluate symptoms of cybersickness experienced during the session. The questionnaire comprises nine items rated on a four-point scale ranging from 0 (“Not at all”) to 3 (“Severe”). Consistent with the original scoring procedure, two subscale scores were computed: the Oculomotor score, obtained by summing the responses to items related to visual discomfort and fatigue, and the Disorientation score, obtained by summing the responses to items related to dizziness and spatial disorientation. A total VRSQ score was then calculated by normalising and combining the two subscale scores according to the method proposed by Kim et al. (2018) \cite{KIM201866}, resulting in a score ranging from 0 to 100. Higher scores indicate a greater degree of VR-induced sickness.

All of these scores, as well as the age and sex of the participant, were saved in a .txt file and later used for statistical analysis.
